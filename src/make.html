How we made this site

A company website is the front door to any modern business, a unique platform on which to promote ideas, content and identity.
As Buildit formed, a new website was required to represent the company in the global marketplace.

The process of building buildit.digital was an interesting process and the opportunity to apply many of Buildit's core concepts.

Given the technical challenges and incremental development we felt the journey might be of interest to a wider audience.

This is our journey of delivering the web site, detailing the challenges and technical achievements that lead to the birth of a new website and brand.

Why are we doing this?

So where to begin? Before starting the team decided on the founding goals of the website.

These goals would guide a design that would support the company message.

The following goals were outlined:

Be an "anchor" that lets the world know Buildit exists.
Help recruitment by attracting candidates and publishing job specs.
Share white papers with the world to showcase our skills and knowledge.
Be a place to start / participate in discussions (e.g. via a blog).

The team

In line with Buildit core principals a small cross functional team was formed to work on the site.

The team consisted of designers, engineers, scrum masters, creative technologists and UX specialists working in agile sprints leading to an incremental delivery of features.

Early architectural decisions

Following an introductory meeting the team made some early decisions regarding the architectural principals for the proposed site:

A living style guide - Buildit is a transformation studio, delivering great user experience with solid engineering principals. Dialog between developer and designer is a crucial part of transforming Buildit customers. A living style guide would allow designers and engineers to collaborate closely using real code and assets rather than static design interpretation. The style guide facilitates clear communication of ideas, a physical repository of assets and the opportunity to reuse code.

Modern website with modern code standards - The website should adhere to as many modern standards as possible without sacrificing usability or access.

Progressive enhancement and graceful degradation - The website should fail gracefully when rendered on devices or browsers incapable of displaying the intended content.

Design similar to our sister company, Designit - within Wipro, Buildit combines into great partnerships, Buildit, Designit and Wipro digital. Designit maintained an existing website. Therefore, as a starting point, it was decided to adopt Designit's general styling and evolve it as the new brand developed.

Project growth and enhancements in an agile fashion - A founding principal of Buildit is to utilise Continuous Delivery and automation. As the site would be an evolutionary process, a pipeline would be required, early on, to facilitate releases. This would mean iterations could be developed, demonstrated and published in gradual increments.

What is a living style guide?

One of the first tasks was to produce the basis for a living style guide.

A living style guide is design implemented as functional code.

This is the complete opposite of more traditional design methods, with assets delivered as pictures or manifests. A living style guide has the huge advantage in that designs can be realised and demonstrated as they would be viewed in production (the same code). It also means designers and developers can communicate fluently in a single, physical design space. Designs represented in a living style guide cannot be lost in translation or scattered over requirements documents. All designs reside and grow in a single style guide, which is used directly by all team members and downstream products.

Atomic Design

To aid development, Atomic Design and CSS principals were employed from the start. Atomic principals, conceived by Brad Frost, break potentially complex design into smaller building blocks.

These building blocks are then used to compose a full design. This concept, especially when combined with a living style guide, makes design easier to understand, test and reuse.

Patternlab: an Open Source Living Style Guide

Patternlab is an open source tool facilitating Atomic design. It allows designers / developers to write, demonstrate and share components. Operating on NodeJs, PatternLab is simple, lightweight and powerful. It also provides the means to start work with minimal cost and effort.

Please visit the Buildit style guide, implemented using Patternlab, to view all the Atomic elements comprising the Buildit website.
